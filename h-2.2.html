mycols=['prestige','degree','padeg','madeg','sex','race','income','rincome','income77','income72']
df_gss_sample_ten_cols=df_gss_sample[mycols]

These variables relate to each other because they all relate to the respondent's socio-economic class and their education. 'degree' is the respondent's highest education, which is influenced by parent's education ('padeg','madeg'), parent's income ('income72','income77') and demographic factors such as 'sex' and 'race'. These variables/ factors also influence a person's socio-economic class, such as 'prestige' and 'income'.

  • How does gss_sample relate to gss_full_row_counts? If we assume gss_sample is a sample of a larger dataset of GSS data, why would we want some information about the full rows counts from the dataset?
  • Generally speaking, what information is contained in the gss_years_per_var and gss_data_dictionary DataFrames? How can we use these sources to better understand our sample?

'get_full_row_counts' gets number of observations for each year in gss_sample dataset. We want this information because it helps us understand whether the the sample is representative compare to the whole population. 'gss_year_per_var' shows whether the variable is collected in a specific year. 'false' shows that the data is missing for that year, and vice versa. Knowing the years when the data is collected help us identify the trends of the data and ensures the consistency in longitudinal analysis. 'gss_data_dictionary' provides explanation for all the variables in the dataset. It helps us understand the context and interprey the variables more accurately.
